##Do NFL teams need to stop the run or the pass in order to win games?

Legendary College Football and NFL coach Chip Kelly once said, "You have to run the football and stop the run, no matter what level you play at, whether it’s high school, college or professional ranks. I’m a firm believer in that.” This idealogy that football teams need to stop the opposing team's running game is a common idea held by many NFL coaches, announcers, proffessional analysts, and fans that dates back to the origin of the game. In fact in 2013, there was a great article on the official SBNation fansite of the Dallas Cowboys detailing the importance of defending the run. Due to its percieved importance there are many videos like this made by the best NFL players detailing how and why to stop the run. Clearly defending the run has the perception of being an integral ingredient to winning football games, however, there has also been a rapidly growing belief that the NFL is becoming "a passing league". Whether its one of the leading news sources in Bleacher Report making the claim or the NFL's official site the football world has seemingly moved toward a pass oriented era. This revolution of football thought has challenged the perception that in order to win stopping the run is of the utmost importance. With the evergrowing belief that the league revolves around the QB (Quarterback) position there is a renewed belief that teams actually need to stop the pass to win games. Teams have begun to pay premium money to Defensive Backs in order to effectively prioritize stopping the pass. We are going to challenge both commonly held beliefs that a team needs to stop the run, and/or stop the pass by approaching the question from purely a statistical standpoint. But before we begin, for any non NFL fans or anyone new to the game of football I highly recommend checking out this link or this one for a quick and easy to understand walk through of how the game works.

Now let's begin and find the answer to the question: Do NFL teams need to stop the run, the pass, or both in order to win games?

Data Scraping and Cleaning Part Two

Despite the lack of success showing a correlation between teams that effectively stop the run or pass and winnning, there is a deeper dive that we as data analysts can still do. Since the Data we were using was cumulative passing and rushing yards and touchdowns, the data did not account the volume of passing or rushing attempts against each team. For example, a team like the Chicago Bears was considered to be in the best third of teams against the pass, however, they encountered the least pass attempts. This may skew our data as it represents the Bears as an elite defensive team against the pass when in fact they just were not tested so thoroughly. In order to accomodate for this possible bias we will create four new columns detailing rushing/passing yards/touchdowns allowed once again, but this time per attempt.

stats_df['RushYds/Att'] = stats_df['RushYds']/stats_df['RushAtt']

How integral is stopping the run or pass to becoming a playoff team. Conclusion

In conclusion, the answer to our question is no. NFL teams do not need to stop the run or pass to win more games. Through our scatter plots and OLS regression analysis we were able to see that neither stopping the run or pass contributed to winning. However, the more complicated answer is yes, as although teams stopping the pass or run may not correlate to winning games it did seem to correlate with making the playoffs which most NFL teams would consider more aligned with winning than actual games won. This was backed up by playoff teams consistently performing better against the run and pass.
